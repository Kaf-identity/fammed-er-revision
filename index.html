1. Family Medicine Principles & Consultation Skills

Core principles of family practice plus the consultation, communication, counselling, EBM and screening frameworks.

Family Medicine (Definition & Principles)

Definition Medical specialty providing continuing and comprehensive care for the individual and family, integrating biological, clinical and behavioral sciences across all ages, both sexes, every organ system and disease entity.
MCQ pearls
  • "Treats the patient who has the disease, not just the disease" → defining ethos; care of the whole patient is the primary goal.
  • Roles of the family physician → care provider, decision-maker, communicator, community leader, team leader (5 roles).
  • First-contact physician acting as gatekeeper / manager of resources → controls admissions, investigations, prescribing and referral.
  • Of all illnesses in a community, 3 of 4 are self-managed; only 1 in 4 reach a primary care doctor, and only ~1 in 10 of those are referred to hospital.
  • Stem "single personal physician, most logical & economical management" → answer = primary care is the most cost-effective system.

MCQ pearls
  • Pendleton model stem → "define reason for attendance incl. nature/history, etiology, and the patient's ideas, concerns & expectations (ICE)".
  • Roger Neighbour's inner consultation → Connecting, Summarising, Handing over, Safety-netting, Housekeeping ("am I in good enough shape for the next patient?").
  • Stott & Davies → management of presenting problem, modification of help-seeking behaviour, management of continuing problems, opportunistic health promotion.
  • Management headings mnemonic RAPRIOP → Reassurance, Advice, Prescription, Referral, Investigation, Observation, Prevention.
  • Breaking bad news → give a "warning shot" ("I'm afraid it looks rather serious") then pause; allow denial as a coping mechanism.

MCQ pearls
  • 90% of complaints about doctors relate to diagnosis, of which 82% are due to history (exam 9%, investigations 9%).
  • A good history gives a correct diagnosis in >50% of all patients and up to 80% in primary care.
  • Average time before the doctor first interrupts the patient = 18 seconds.
  • Video recording = method of choice for monitoring consultation communication skills.
  • Open vs closed vs leading questions → leading questions are "best avoided" (e.g. "would you say you get loose motions after spicy food?").

Counselling

Definition Structured patient education/reassurance for a chronic condition: introduce yourself & take permission, establish "what you know and what you want to know", then explain disease, investigations, and management.
Red flags In an IBS counselling stem, weight loss, IDA, age >50, melena/hematemesis, persistent vomiting, jaundice, or family Hx of IBD/celiac/cancer mandate exclusion of organic disease before labelling it IBS.
MCQ pearls
  • IBS → diagnosed by Rome IV criteria (recurrent abdominal pain ≥1 day/week × 3 months, linked to defecation / stool frequency / stool form); functional, "manageable but not curable".
  • DM diagnosis cut-offs → HbA1c ≥6.5%, FPG ≥126 mg/dL, OGTT/random ≥200 mg/dL; pre-diabetes HbA1c 5.7–6.4%; target HbA1c <7%.
  • Metformin = first oral DM2 drug → ↓ hepatic glucose output, no hypos, weight loss; C/I in renal impairment; S/E B12 deficiency, lactic acidosis.
  • HTN management target <140/90; DASH diet salt <5 g/day; ACEI preferred in diabetics (S/E cough, angioedema, hyperkalemia).
  • IDA → ferrous sulphate on empty stomach, take with lemon/vitamin C (↑ absorption), avoid Ca/tea/coffee; check reticulocytes at day 10.

MCQ pearls
  • Gold standard for a therapeutic question = prospectively designed double-blind randomized clinical trial; meta-analysis/systematic review = highest level of evidence.
  • 5 A's of practising EBM → Asking, Accessing, Appraising, Applying, Assessing.
  • Appraisal assesses three things → Validity (closeness to truth), Impact (size of effect), Applicability.
  • Usefulness of information = (relevance × validity) ÷ work.
  • Quality improvement = reducing underuse, overuse and misuse; ~20% of what we do can be safely eliminated.

Periodic Health Assessment & Screening

Definition Systematic program to prevent illness, maintain function and promote health via immunizations, cancer screening and screening for common diseases.
LevelPrimarySecondaryTertiary
AimPrevent disease before it occursEarly detection (screening)Management once diagnosed
ExampleImmunization, removal of colon polypsMammography for breast cancerRehabilitation after stroke
MCQ pearls
  • Wilson's screening criteria → condition is an important problem with a latent/asymptomatic phase, a treatment exists, an acceptable test exists, natural history understood, case-finding is continuous (not "once and for all").
  • Ideal screening test = highly sensitive (few false negatives) and highly specific (few false positives), safe, cheap, acceptable.
  • USPSTF grades → A & B = recommend, C = clinician's judgment, D = recommend against, I = insufficient evidence; A/B grades = "gold standard".
  • Cervical cancer → start at age 21; cytology every 3 yrs (21–29); co-testing every 5 yrs (30–65).
  • Lung cancer screening → LDCT at age 50 in ≥30 pack-year smokers (or quit <15 yrs); AAA → one-time ultrasound in men >65 who ever smoked.

Test Yourself — Family Medicine Principles & Consultation Skills

Q1. In a community of 1000 people experiencing illness in a given month, roughly how many are seen by a primary care physician, with the rest self-managing?

  1. About 1 in 2
  2. About 1 in 4
  3. About 1 in 10
  4. About 3 in 4
Answer: B. The sheet states 3 of 4 illnesses are self-managed and only 1 in 4 reach a primary care physician; option C (1 in 10) is the further fraction of those who are then referred to hospital.

Q2. A trainee is assessed on a consultation model that explicitly includes a "housekeeping" stage — checking the doctor is in good enough shape for the next patient. Which model is this?

  1. Pendleton model
  2. Stott & Davies model
  3. Roger Neighbour's inner consultation
  4. Health belief model
Answer: C. Housekeeping is one of Neighbour's stages (Connecting, Summarising, Handing over, Safety-netting, Housekeeping). Pendleton focuses on defining attendance and ICE; it has no "housekeeping" step.

Q3. According to the communication sheet, the single largest share of human communication is carried by which channel?

  1. The actual words spoken (verbal)
  2. Tone of voice
  3. Body language
  4. Written notes
Answer: C. 93% of communication is non-verbal — body language 55% and tone of voice 38% — leaving only 7% for the actual words.

Q4. A study reports its outcome as a reduction in HbA1c, a surrogate laboratory value, rather than a change in mortality or quality of life. This is best classified as which type of evidence?

  1. POEM (patient-oriented evidence that matters)
  2. DOE (disease-oriented evidence)
  3. A systematic review
  4. Level A USPSTF recommendation
Answer: B. DOE deals with surrogate endpoints (e.g. blood sugar, BP, flow rate) that may or may not reflect real patient outcomes; POEM measures morbidity, mortality, symptoms, QoL or cost.

Q5. A new program proposes mass screening for a disease. To satisfy Wilson's criteria, which feature of the disease is essential?

  1. It must be rapidly fatal once symptoms appear
  2. It must have a recognisable latent or asymptomatic stage
  3. It must have no available treatment
  4. The screening test should be highly invasive
Answer: B. Wilson's criteria require a latent/asymptomatic stage so early detection is useful; a treatment must exist (not C) and the test should be acceptable/non-invasive (not D).

Diagnosis-by-numbers: know the cut-offs cold.

Red flags Emergency = SBP ≥180 and/or DBP ≥110 + end-organ damage (reduce MAP by 25% over 1–2 h, IV agents). Urgency = SBP ≥180/DBP ≥120 without end-organ damage (lower over 24 h, oral agents). Most commonly due to non-compliance.
MCQ pearls
  • Single visit diagnoses HTN if ≥200/120, or ≥140/90 with end-organ damage.
  • First-line agents (all equal): thiazide, ACEI, ARB, CCB; β-blockers are 2nd line.
  • Diabetic / CKD → ACEI or ARB (renoprotective; may ↑ creatinine in RAS — bilateral RAS is a contraindication).
  • African-American or aged >55 → thiazide or CCB; HF → ACEI + β-blocker.
  • Pregnancy → methyldopa, nifedipine, or labetalol (ACEI/ARB contraindicated).
  • Thiazide stem: "hyperglycemia/uricemia/lipidemia/calcemia + hypokalemia/natremia" → gout flare points away from thiazide.
  • ACEI dry cough/angioedema → switch to ARB.

MCQ pearls
  • Diagnosis: HbA1c ≥6.5%, FPG ≥126 mg/dL, OGTT 2-h ≥200, or random ≥200 + symptoms. With symptoms → 1 abnormal test; without → 2 abnormal tests.
  • Prediabetes: HbA1c 5.7–6.4%, FPG 100–125 (IFG), OGTT 140–199.
  • Targets: BP <140/90, LDL <100 (<70 high-risk), FBS <130, post-prandial <180, HbA1c <7.
  • First-line drug = Metformin (no weight gain, no hypoglycaemia; risk: B12 deficiency, lactic acidosis; avoid in renal impairment).
  • Weight loss drugs = metformin, SGLT2, GLP-1; hypoglycaemia = sulfonylureas & meglitinides.
  • Retinal screen: T1DM at 5 years from diagnosis, T2DM at diagnosis.
  • Microalbuminuria = 30–300 mg/day (early nephropathy).

MCQ pearls
  • LDL (calculated) = TC − (HDL + TG/5); fasting needed for TG.
  • Most effective: LDL → statin; TG → fibrate or niacin; HDL → niacin.
  • CHD equivalents: DM, symptomatic carotid stenosis, PAD, AAA.
  • Statin counselling: take at night, baseline LFT & CPK, no grapefruit juice; absolute C/I = acute/chronic liver disease.
  • Familial hypercholesterolaemia (AD, 1 in 500): ↑↑ LDL, tendon xanthomas, premature CHD.
  • Metabolic syndrome (any 3): central obesity, TG ≥150, low HDL (M<40/F<50), BP ≥130/85, FPG ≥100.

  • Hashimoto's marker = anti-TPO; Graves' marker = TSH-receptor antibodies.

  • Pregnancy: add 2 extra levothyroxine doses/week once confirmed; check TSH every 4 weeks.
  • Radio-iodine worsens Graves' ophthalmopathy (especially smokers); de Quervain's = viral, fever + ↑ESR, self-limiting.

  • Central obesity by waist: ≥102 cm men / ≥88 cm women (USA/Europe); WHR risk if ≥1.0.

  • Diet: 500 kcal/day deficit → 1–2 lb/week loss; >5% loss improves CVD risk.

Test Yourself — Cardiometabolic & Endocrine

Q1. A <45-year-old man> has a single office reading of 185/115 mmHg and presents with new-onset blurred vision and papilledema on fundoscopy. What is the single best immediate step?

  1. Re-check BP in one week and start a thiazide
  2. Reduce mean arterial pressure by 25% over 1–2 hours with IV agents
  3. Start oral amlodipine and review in 24 hours
  4. Reassure; this is white-coat hypertension
Answer: B. SBP ≥180/DBP ≥110 with end-organ damage (papilledema) = hypertensive emergency → lower MAP by 25% over 1–2 h with IV agents. Option C (24 h oral) describes urgency, which has no end-organ damage.

Q2. A <19-year-old> with a 2-week history of polyuria, polydipsia and weight loss has ketonuria and a very low insulin level. Which feature most strongly points to Type 1 rather than Type 2 DM?

  1. Strong family history
  2. Onset over months to years
  3. Presence of autoantibodies with insulitis
  4. Normal or high insulin level
Answer: C. T1DM is autoimmune (autoantibodies, insulitis, very low insulin, ketosis-prone). Options A, B and D describe Type 2 DM.

Q3. A <55-year-old man> with established coronary heart disease has an LDL of 145 mg/dL despite lifestyle changes. According to the sheet's targets, what LDL goal should you aim for?

  1. <70 mg/dL (preferred for CHD/equivalent)
  2. <130 mg/dL
  3. <160 mg/dL
  4. <190 mg/dL
Answer: A. CHD or CHD-equivalent patients have an LDL goal <100, and <70 is preferred. <130 and <160 apply to the 2+ and 0–1 risk-factor categories respectively.

Q4. A <30-year-old woman> has heat intolerance, palpitations, weight loss despite increased appetite, and a diffuse smooth goiter. Labs show suppressed TSH. Which antibody confirms the most likely cause?

  1. Anti-thyroid peroxidase (anti-TPO)
  2. TSH-receptor antibodies
  3. Anti-thyroglobulin (anti-Tg)
  4. TSH receptor-blocking antibodies (TBII)
Answer: B. Picture is Graves' disease (commonest cause of hyperthyroidism); TSH-receptor antibodies activate the receptor. Anti-TPO and anti-Tg are typical of Hashimoto's hypothyroidism.

Q5. A patient has a BMI of 41 kg/m² with no response to diet and lifestyle. Per the sheet, what is the most effective intervention for weight loss in this patient?

  1. Orlistat alone
  2. Phentermine/topiramate
  3. Bariatric surgery
  4. Continued behavioural counselling only
Answer: C. Bariatric surgery (offered at BMI ≥40, or 30–39.9 with comorbidities) is the most effective intervention for weight loss and gives the greatest diabetes remission. Medications are adjuncts when diet/lifestyle fail.

  • Centor/McIsaac high score + GABHS → amoxicillin (azithromycin if penicillin-allergic); goal = prevent rheumatic fever (NOT PSGN).

  • Sinusitis antibiotics only if symptoms ≥10 days, fever ≥39°C, or "double-sickening" → amoxicillin/augmentin first-line.
  • Avoid aspirin in children (Reye syndrome); honey for nocturnal cough.

Test Yourself — Common Symptom Presentations

Q1. A 19-year-old presents with sore throat, marked fatigue, and high fever. Exam shows prominent tonsillar exudates, posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and splenomegaly. Most likely diagnosis?

  1. GABHS pharyngitis
  2. Infectious mononucleosis (EBV)
  3. Herpangina
  4. Diphtheria
Answer: B. Posterior cervical nodes + splenomegaly + exudates = EBV mono. GABHS gives anterior cervical nodes and abrupt fever without splenomegaly.

Q2. A 45-year-old with 4 weeks of epigastric pain also reports a 4 kg unintentional weight loss and one episode of melena. What is the best next step?

  1. Empirical PPI and reassess in 4 weeks
  2. Test-and-treat for H. pylori
  3. Upper GI endoscopy
  4. Low-FODMAP diet
Answer: C. Weight loss + melena are ALARM features → upper endoscopy regardless of age. Empirical PPI/test-and-treat is only for <60 yr without alarm features.

Q3. A 68-year-old woman has a new unilateral temporal headache, scalp tenderness, jaw pain when chewing, and transient visual blurring. ESR is elevated. Best immediate action?

Three can't-miss derangements of glucose and potassium — diagnose on the numbers, treat in order.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Red flags If K+ <3.5 mmol/L, correct potassium BEFORE starting insulin — insulin drives K+ intracellularly and can precipitate fatal arrhythmia. Bicarbonate is not indicated and may harm.
MCQ pearls
  • Management order = Fluids → Insulin → K+. Fluids are the single most important first intervention (70 kg adult may be ~7 L deficit).
  • Fixed-rate IV insulin infusion at 0.1 units/kg/hr (IV preferred — faster fall in glucose/ketones than SC).
  • Once glucose <14 mmol/L → add 10% dextrose and consider dropping insulin to 0.05 units/kg/hr.
  • K+ replacement when level <5.5 mmol/L with urine output ≥0.5 ml/kg/hr; aim K+ 4.0–5.5. K+ <3.5 → hold insulin, replace K+ first.

  • "Unresponsive after treatment" stem → hypoglycaemia, cerebral oedema, or ARDS (NOT hyperkalaemia — K+ falls with treatment).

  • Whipple's triad = symptoms + low glucose + relief on correction → classic exam definition.
  • Cut-off for treatment in diabetics = <4.0 mmol/L; target post-treatment CBG 5–10 mmol/L.

  • Severe hypoglycaemia = episode requiring the help of another person.
  • IV dextrose stimulates insulin → rebound hypoglycaemia, especially with sulphonylureas — monitor / admit.

Hyperkalaemia

Definition Serum K+ >5.5 mEq/L (normal 3.5–5.5). Severity: mild 5.5–5.9, moderate 6.0–6.4, severe ≥6.5 mEq/L.

MCQ pearls
  • Stepwise order = Stabilise (Ca gluconate) → Shift (insulin+glucose / β-agonist / NaHCO3) → Remove (furosemide / dialysis / binders) → Treat cause.
  • Calcium does NOT lower K+ — it only protects the myocardium; give it first when ECG changes are present.
  • Drug causes of ↓excretion: spironolactone, ACEi/ARBs, NSAIDs; redistribution: acidosis (DKA), insulin deficiency, β-blockers.
  • Production causes: haemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, tumour lysis syndrome.
  • K+ is 98% intracellular; renal route = 90% of excretion — think renal failure in unexplained hyperkalaemia.
  • Suspect pseudohyperkalaemia (falsely high sample) if the number doesn't fit the clinical picture.

11. Toxicology, Anaphylaxis & Environmental

Toxidromes and antidotes, IM-adrenaline-first anaphylaxis, and heat/cold/drowning/choking emergencies.

Poisoning

Definition Dose-related adverse effects following exposure to chemicals or drugs; may be local (skin, eyes, lungs) or systemic. Ingestion is the most common route.
MCQ pearls
  • Treating the poisoned patient (ABC, supportive care) is more important than identifying the agent; observe poison cases 24–48 hours before discharge.
  • Toxidromes read off vitals + end-organ signs: mental status, pupil size, peristalsis, skin (dry vs diaphoretic).
  • Pinpoint pupils + ↓RR + ↓consciousness → opioid toxidrome → give naloxone.

Definition Heat-related illness ranges from heat oedema to heat stroke (thermoregulatory failure); drowning = respiratory impairment from submersion/immersion; choking = foreign-body airway obstruction.
