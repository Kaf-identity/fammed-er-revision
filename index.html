1. Family Medicine Principles & Consultation Skills

First-contact, continuous, comprehensive, coordinated care — and the evidence base behind it.

Family Medicine (Principles) DEEP

Definition The specialty in breadth providing continuing and comprehensive health care for the individual and family, integrating biological, clinical and behavioural sciences across all ages, both sexes, every organ system and every disease entity.
Clinical The family physician wears five hats: care provider, decision maker, communicator, community leader, team leader. The patient is treated as an integral part of family and community — "treat the patient who has the disease, not just the disease".
PrincipleMeaning
ComprehensiveAvailable for any problem, either sex, any age
ContinuousCare is total and continuing — not terminated by cure or incurability; longitudinal record
CoordinatedFirst-contact "gatekeeper"; controls referral, investigations, admissions; central source of pt information
AccountablePatient advocate; educates on outcome/prognosis; understands pt preferences
ContextualIllness understood only in personal, family & social context
FeatureInpatient settingOutpatient (FM) setting
RelationshipPaternalistic; orders written; pt often can't name doctorHealth advisor/counsellor; educate, persuade, negotiate
DisordersMedicalized, differentiatedUndifferentiated; nonspecific (tiredness, dizziness)
Cost / approachCostlyLess costly; stepwise use of time; ~50% psychosocial
MCQ pearls
  • Core principles = comprehensive, continuous, coordinated care (NOT hospital-based, NOT women/children only, NOT surgical, NOT preventive-only).
  • Roles of FP: provider, decision maker, communicator, community leader, team leader — specialised surgical procedures are NOT a role.
  • Quality of a country's health care correlates with primary-care performance.
  • Of all illness in a community, 3 in 4 are self-managed; only 1 in 4 reaches a primary-care physician; only ~1 in 10 of those is referred to hospital.
  • FM physician has a higher tolerance for uncertainty than the consultant; expert at rapid first-time assessment.
  • Primary care = keystone, accessible, affordable, comprehensive, coordinated, continuous, accountable — promotes health over the long term (not short term).
  • Ethical pillars: justice/equity, beneficence, respect for persons (autonomy, informed consent, truth-telling, confidentiality).

Evidence-Based Medicine DEEP

Definition The integration of best research evidence with clinical expertise and patient values (all three components — not just research + expertise).
5 steps (the 5 A's) Ask (form an answerable question) → Access (track down best evidence) → Appraise (validity, impact, applicability) → Apply (integrate with expertise + patient values) → Assess (evaluate & improve your own performance).
LevelEvidence (strongest → weakest)
StrongestMeta-analysis / systematic review — highest level ever achieved

ObservationalCohort > case-control > cross-sectional > case series
WeakestExpert opinion / consensus (textbooks out of date; experts frequently wrong)
FeaturePOEM (Patient-Oriented Evidence)DOE (Disease-Oriented Evidence)
MeasuresOutcomes that matter to patientsSurrogate end-points
ExamplesMorbidity, mortality, symptoms, QoL, costBlood sugar, BP, flow rate, plaque thickness, lab values

MCQ pearls
  • RCT = highest level of evidence for effectiveness of a treatment; meta-analysis = strongest evidence overall; expert/consensus opinion = weakest.
  • EBM definition has THREE parts (research evidence + clinical expertise + patient values) — "research + expertise only" is FALSE.
  • POEM > DOE in reliability for guiding care.
  • Useful information = (relevance × validity) ÷ work.
  • Value in health care = quality ÷ cost; quality improved by reducing under-use, over-use, misuse.
  • "Half of what you're taught is wrong within 10 years — and no one knows which half" → rationale for EBM as lifelong self-directed learning.
  • Appraise evidence for validity (truth), impact (effect size), applicability (usefulness).
Mnemonic: 5 A's — Ask, Access, Appraise, Apply, Assess.

Consultation SUMMARISED

Definition The central act of medicine — two parts: the interview (why the patient came) and the exposition (diagnosis, treatment, advice). The opening puts the patient at ease; you don't get a second chance at a first impression.
StyleDoctor-centeredPatient-centered (preferred)
AuthorityDoctor decides; patient passivePatient active & involved
FocusSymptoms onlySymptoms + ideas, concerns, expectations
MCQ pearls
  • Doctor-centered is NOT the preferred style — patient-centered is.
  • Patient ideas = what the patient thinks about the nature/cause of the complaint; concerns = worries; expectations = what they hope for.
  • Pendleton model = defines reason for attendance (nature, aetiology, ideas/concerns/expectations, effects).
  • Management headings = RAPRIOP: Reassurance, Advice, Prescription, Referral, Investigation, Observation, Prevention.
  • Breaking bad news: preparation → find out what pt knows → warning shot → allow denial → explain if requested → listen → plan → offer availability.

Communication SUMMARISED

Definition Multi-level information exchange. Aims: to be heard & accepted, to be understood, to get action — if any aim fails, communication has not occurred.
ChannelShare of communication
Body language (non-verbal)55%

MCQ pearls
  • 93% of communication is non-verbal (55% body language + 38% tone); only 7% is words.
  • Most complaints about doctors (~90%) relate to diagnosis, mostly history (82%) — i.e. failure of communication, not poor medicine.
  • History gives a correct diagnosis in >50% of all patients and up to 80% in primary care.
  • Method of choice for monitoring consultation communication skills = video recording.
  • ~1/3 of primary-care patients have no organic disease to account for symptoms.
  • When manner and content are incongruous, the non-verbal message is more truthful (verbal is less truthful — common EXCEPT trap).
  • Average doctor interrupts the patient after only 18 seconds.
  • Facilitators: nodding, leaning forward, eye contact, reflecting, clarifying, summarizing, silences.

Counselling SUMMARISED

Definition Structured patient education + behaviour-change support. Open with "introduce yourself and take permission; find out what you know and what you want to know"; frame disease (common? curable? serious?), investigations, complications, prognosis, then management (lifestyle first, then drugs).
MCQ pearls
  • Counselling formula = introduce → assess knowledge → explain disease → lifestyle modification → medications → red flags.
  • ~50% of all medical advice is not followed; compliance improves when the patient feels in control, is involved in decisions, and advice fits their health beliefs.
  • Health Belief Model components: health motivation, perceived vulnerability, perceived seriousness, perceived cost — triggered by cues to action.
  • Counselling depends on good communication as its cornerstone.

Test Yourself — Family Medicine Principles & Consultation Skills

Q1. A medical student is asked to define the core principles that distinguish family medicine from other specialties. Which statement best captures these principles?

  1. A specialty focused on hospital-based acute care
  2. A specialty providing comprehensive, continuous and coordinated care
  3. A specialty limited to women and children
  4. A specialty providing mainly surgical interventions
  5. A specialty focusing only on preventive medicine
Answer: B. FM is defined by comprehensive, continuous, coordinated (and contextual, accountable) care for all ages and both sexes. The others describe narrow or hospital-bound practice, the opposite of FM's breadth.

Q2. All of the following are TRUE regarding evidence-based medicine EXCEPT:

  1. For patients it represents a more effective use of resources
  2. Patient-oriented evidence (POEM) is more reliable than disease-oriented evidence (DOE)
  3. Meta-analysis provides the strongest evidence available in medicine
  4. EBM improves clinicians' confidence in managing clinical situations
  5. EBM is defined as the integration of only two components: best research evidence and clinical expertise
Answer: E. EBM has THREE components — best research evidence, clinical expertise AND patient values. Dropping patient values makes the statement false. A–D are all true.

Q3. A researcher wants the highest level of evidence for the effectiveness of a new antihypertensive drug. Which single study design provides this?

  1. Case-control study
  2. Case series
  3. Cross-sectional study
  4. Randomized controlled trial
  5. Cohort study
Answer: D. The prospective double-blind RCT is the gold standard for therapeutic decisions. Cohort and case-control are observational (lower); case series and cross-sectional are weaker still. A meta-analysis of RCTs would rank higher, but is not a single study design offered here.

Q4. All of the following are TRUE regarding the consultation and communication EXCEPT:

  1. The opening of the consultation is key to putting the patient at ease
  2. More than 80% of diagnoses can be made by history taking alone in primary care
  3. The doctor-centered style is the preferred consultation style
  4. About 93% of communication is non-verbal
  5. Video recording is the method of choice for monitoring consultation skills
Answer: C. The patient-centered style is preferred, not doctor-centered. A, B, D and E are all true per the sheets (history yields the diagnosis in up to 80% in primary care; 93% of communication is non-verbal; video is the monitoring method of choice).

Q5. A 50-year-old asymptomatic woman with no risk factors asks when she should begin screening. According to the periodic health assessment guidance used here, which statement is correct?

  1. Cervical cytology should have begun at age 21
  2. Cervical screening should only begin now at age 50
  3. Mammography is not indicated at any age
  4. Routine breast self-examination is the recommended screening modality
  5. Colorectal cancer screening is not needed until age 60
Answer: A. Cervical screening starts at 21, so by 50 she is well within the screening window. Mammography starts at 40 (so it IS indicated); breast self-exam is level D (recommend against); colorectal screening starts at 45, not 60.

2. Cardiometabolic & Endocrine

The most heavily tested family-medicine block — HTN, DM and dyslipidemia first, then thyroid and obesity.

Hypertension

Clinical Usually asymptomatic. Severe elevation → severe headache, nausea/vomiting, chest pain, dyspnoea, dizziness, blurred vision. Resistant HTN = uncontrolled despite 3 drugs of different classes. White-coat = high office/normal home; masked = normal office/high home.

MCQ pearls
  • ACEI → dry cough (switch to ARB); amlodipine/CCB → bilateral ankle oedema.
  • Secondary HTN by age: child = glomerulonephritis; adolescent = coarctation of aorta; >65 = renal artery stenosis (MC overall renovascular/renal parenchymal).
  • ACEI/ARB preferred in diabetics; pregnancy → methyldopa, nifedipine, labetalol.
  • Routine basic work-up does NOT include thyroid/cortisol unless clinically suspected.
  • NSAIDs, steroids, OCP, decongestants, alcohol/caffeine can raise BP (naproxen safest NSAID).
  • Follow-up: K & creatinine 1–2×/yr; yearly ECG, FBS, lipids, urinalysis.

  • Metformin does NOT cause hypoglycemia and does NOT cause weight gain (often causes mild loss).

  • Strong evidence glycemic control reduces microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy); BP control reduces micro- AND macrovascular events.
  • Diabetic LDL target <100 (DM is a CHD equivalent); BP target <140/90.
  • SGLT2 → genital candidiasis/UTI; GLP-1 → weight loss, nausea; sulfonylureas → hypoglycemia.

MCQ pearls
  • Statins are first-line for high LDL (most effective LDL-lowering drug).
  • Hypertriglyceridemia (TG ≥500) → acute pancreatitis risk.
  • Diabetic LDL target <100 (CHD equivalent → often <70).
  • Secondary hyperlipidemia: hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, obesity, alcohol, pregnancy, obstructive liver disease; drugs = thiazide, β-blockers, OCP, steroids.
  • CHD equivalents: DM, symptomatic carotid stenosis, PAD, AAA.
  • Stop statin if CPK >10× normal or LFT >3× normal → switch to e.g. niacin.
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (AD) → tendon xanthomas, premature CHD.

MCQ pearls
  • Hypothyroidism: ↑ TSH + ↓ T4; anti-TPO is the most detected antibody; women 10× > men.

  • Hyperthyroidism: weight loss with increased appetite, tremor, lid lag; pretibial myxedema is specific for Graves.
  • Levothyroxine: once daily AM, ≥30 min before food/caffeine/calcium/iron; in pregnancy add 2 extra doses/week.
  • Hypothyroidism causes secondary dyslipidemia (a tested EXCEPT-stem trap).

  • Obesity = BMI ≥30 (≥25 is overweight) — a common distractor swap.

  • Bariatric surgery (BMI ≥40, or ≥30–39.9 with comorbidity) = most effective; gastric bypass = MC worldwide, greatest loss.
  • 500 kcal/day deficit → 1–2 lb/wk; ≥5% weight loss improves CVD risk.

Test Yourself — Cardiometabolic & Endocrine

Q1. A 58-year-old man started on an antihypertensive develops a persistent dry cough at night that resolves on stopping the drug. Which class is most likely responsible, and what is the best alternative?

  1. Thiazide → switch to CCB
  2. CCB → switch to ACEI
  3. ACE inhibitor → switch to ARB
  4. β-blocker → switch to thiazide
  5. ARB → switch to ACE inhibitor
Answer: C. ACEIs cause a dry cough (and angioedema); ARBs share the mechanism without cough/angioedema. CCBs cause ankle oedema, not cough.

Q2. All of the following are true regarding diabetes mellitus EXCEPT:

  1. Diagnosis can be made if HbA1c is ≥6.5%
  2. Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia
  3. Metformin commonly causes weight gain
  4. BP control reduces both micro- and macrovascular events
  5. A fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL is diagnostic
Answer: C. Metformin is weight-neutral to weight-reducing, never causing gain; sulfonylureas and thiazolidinediones cause weight gain. The others are all correct diagnostic/management facts.

Q3. A 44-year-old man has triglycerides of 820 mg/dL. Besides cardiovascular risk, which acute complication is he at greatest risk of, and what is the priority management?

  1. Acute pancreatitis → very-low-fat diet plus a fibrate
  2. Acute MI → high-intensity statin alone
  3. Gout flare → allopurinol
  4. Hepatic failure → stop all lipid drugs
  5. Stroke → start aspirin
Answer: A. TG ≥500 mg/dL markedly raises acute pancreatitis risk; management is a very-low-fat diet with fibrate or niacin to lower TG first. Statins are most effective for LDL, not the priority here.

Q4. Which of the following is FALSE regarding hypothyroidism?

  1. The most common cause is Hashimoto thyroiditis
  2. Laboratory findings are ↑ TSH and ↓ free T4
  3. It can cause secondary hyperlipidemia
  4. Subclinical disease shows ↑ TSH with normal T3/T4
  5. Routine thyroid ultrasound is always required to confirm it
Answer: E. Diagnosis is biochemical (↑ TSH, ↓ T4); ultrasound/radionuclide scanning is not routinely needed. The other statements are all true.

Q5. A 60-year-old man with type 2 diabetes and hypertension has an LDL of 162 mg/dL. Which statement about his lipid management is most appropriate?

  1. His LDL goal is <160 mg/dL because he has no symptoms
  2. His LDL goal is <100 mg/dL (or <70) as diabetes is a CHD equivalent, and a statin is first-line
  3. A fibrate is the first-line agent for elevated LDL
  4. Statins should be taken in the morning for best effect
  5. Lifestyle change alone for 3 months is sufficient before any drug
Answer: B. Diabetes is a CHD equivalent, so the LDL goal is <100 (often <70); statins are first-line and most effective for LDL. Fibrates target TG; statins are taken at night.

3. Common Symptom Presentations

High-frequency primary-care complaints — when to reassure, when to investigate.

Dyspepsia

Definition Discomfort or pain in the upper abdomen (± epigastric fullness, nausea, vomiting, heartburn), especially after eating/drinking. More common in women, smokers, NSAID users.
Clinical Functional (non-ulcer) = 75% (no cause on evaluation); Organic = 25%. Organic GI causes: GERD, PUD, gastritis, gastric/esophageal cancer, biliary pain, pancreatitis. Non-GI: DM, thyroid disease, hyperparathyroidism, severe renal disease, drugs (NSAIDs, antibiotics, estrogen). Exam usually normal except epigastric tenderness — which cannot distinguish organic from functional.
Red flags (ALARM features) Unexplained iron-deficiency anemia; haematemesis/melena; unexplained weight loss >3 kg; progressive dysphagia/odynophagia; persistent vomiting; family history of GI malignancy; palpable abdominal mass or lymphadenopathy.
Endoscopy decisionAge ≥60Age <60
Upper endoscopyRecommended (all)Only if alarm feature present
Triggers (<60)—Wt loss >5% over 6–12 mo; overt GI bleed; >1 alarm feature; rapidly progressive alarm features; persistent dyspepsia despite Rx
Diagnosis — H. pylori testing Non-invasive: urea breath test = most accurate; serology (antibodies); fecal antigen. Invasive (biopsy at gastroscopy, more expensive): rapid urease/CLO test, histology, culture. Test those who don't need endoscopy, or in whom biopsy wasn't taken.
Treatment <60 y, no alarm features: start empirical PPI while awaiting H. pylori result. If positive → eradication. If negative or symptoms persist → low-dose TCA or prokinetic.
H. pylori triple therapy (1st line, 1 wk): PPI BD + amoxicillin (or metronidazole) + clarithromycin. Quadruple (2nd line, 2 wk): PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline.
MCQ pearls
  • Functional dyspepsia = diagnosis of exclusion (≥1 mo epigastric discomfort, normal endoscopy). Rome IV: dyspepsia >3 mo within last 6 mo, no organic cause, not related to defecation. Subtypes: EPS (epigastric pain) & PDS (postprandial fullness/early satiation).
  • H. pylori = most common cause of PUD; only 20–25% with suggestive symptoms actually have an ulcer.
  • Green/viscous discharge does NOT prove bacterial cause; epigastric tenderness does NOT distinguish organic vs functional.
  • GERD: heartburn = cardinal symptom; lifestyle modification is 1st line; PPI 1st-line drug; a normal endoscopy does NOT exclude GERD. Barrett's risk: reflux >10 y, male, obese, smoker, white.
  • IBS (functional, MC in women) overlaps in ~30–35% of functional dyspepsia; Rome IV needs recurrent pain ≥1 day/week ×3 mo + ≥2 of: related to defecation, change in stool frequency, change in stool form. Diagnose only with no alarm symptoms + negative workup (CBC, CRP, fecal calprotectin, celiac serology).
  • Pancreatitis = stabbing pain radiating to back (alcohol); biliary = postprandial pain after fatty meals.

  • 1. Fluids first — appropriate fluid replacement is the most important initial intervention (a 70 kg adult may be ~7 L deficit); restores volume, clears ketones, corrects electrolytes.
  • 2. Potassium check / the K⁺ rule — if K⁺ <3.5 mmol/L, correct it BEFORE starting insulin (insulin drives K⁺ into cells → can precipitate arrhythmia/arrest). Start K⁺ replacement when level <5.5 mmol/L with urine output ≥0.5 ml/kg/hr; target K⁺ 4.0–5.5.
  • 3. Insulin — fixed-rate IV infusion at 0.1 units/kg/hr (IV preferred; faster fall in glucose/ketones than SC). Once glucose <14 mmol/L add 10% dextrose and consider reducing to 0.05 units/kg/hr.
  • 4. Treat the underlying cause (infection 20–25%, first presentation 10–22%, MI 3–6%).
Targets/hr: ketones ↓0.5, HCO₃ ↑3.0, glucose ↓3.0 mmol/L; if not met, ↑ insulin by 1 unit/hr.
Red flags Bicarbonate is NOT indicated (delays lactate/ketone clearance, paradoxical CSF acidosis). Phosphate not routinely given. Watch for hypoglycaemia & hypokalaemia from treatment, and (rarely in adults) cerebral oedema and ARDS.
MCQ pearls
  • Triad of criteria: ketones >3.0, glucose >11.0, HCO₃ <15 / pH <7.3.
  • Fluids before insulin; insulin 0.1 units/kg/hr fixed rate.
  • K⁺ <3.5 → replace potassium before insulin.
  • Plasma K⁺ is normal/high at presentation despite total-body depletion.
  • Sodium bicarbonate is not indicated and may cause harm.
  • Venous sample suffices; ABG only for suspected respiratory failure.
  • Raised WCC may reflect ketone level, not necessarily infection.
Mnemonic: "F-K-I-C" — Fluids, fix K⁺ (if <3.5), Insulin 0.1 u/kg/hr, treat Cause.

Q1. A 19-year-old with type 1 diabetes is unwell with vomiting. Venous gas: glucose 28 mmol/L, pH 7.18, HCO₃ 11 mmol/L, blood ketones 4.2 mmol/L, K⁺ 3.2 mmol/L. Which is the most appropriate first step in management?

  2. Give IV sodium bicarbonate to correct the acidosis
  3. Begin IV fluids and replace potassium before starting insulin
  4. Administer IM glucagon
  5. Give IV calcium gluconate
Answer: C. Fluids are first-line and, with K⁺ <3.5, potassium must be corrected before insulin (insulin drives K⁺ intracellularly → arrhythmia). Bicarbonate is not indicated; glucagon and calcium are not DKA treatments.

Q2. All of the following are diagnostic criteria for DKA EXCEPT:

  1. Blood glucose >11.0 mmol/L (or known diabetes)
  2. Ketonaemia >3.0 mmol/L or ketonuria > +2
  3. Venous HCO₃ <15.0 mmol/L or venous pH <7.3
  4. Serum potassium <3.5 mmol/L
  5. Significant ketonuria on urine dipstick
Answer: D. Low potassium is a treatment consideration, not a diagnostic criterion. The three criteria are hyperglycaemia, ketonaemia/ketonuria, and acidosis (low HCO₃ / pH).

Q3. A 70-year-old on insulin is found drowsy; CBG 2.1 mmol/L. He has IV access and responds poorly. Which is the most appropriate treatment?

  1. Oral glucose tablets
  2. IV 50% dextrose by rapid bolus
  3. IV 10% dextrose (~100 ml)
  4. IM glucagon as sole therapy
  5. Withhold treatment and recheck in 1 hour
Answer: C. With IV access and reduced consciousness, IV 10% dextrose is preferred; 50% is hypertonic and causes inflammation. Oral is unsafe if not fully cooperative; glucagon alone is less reliable and risks recurrence without follow-up carbohydrate.

Q4. A 60-year-old with CKD has K⁺ 6.8 mEq/L and peaked T waves on ECG. Which is the most appropriate first intervention?

  1. Nebulised salbutamol (B-agonist)
  2. IV insulin with glucose
  3. IV calcium gluconate
  4. Oral potassium binder (Kayexalate)
  5. Furosemide
Answer: C. Calcium gluconate stabilises the myocardium first. Shifting agents (insulin/glucose, B-agonist, bicarbonate) come next, then removal (diuretic, binder, dialysis). Calcium itself does not lower the serum K⁺.

Q5. Regarding the management of DKA, which statement is FALSE?

  1. Fluid replacement is the most important initial intervention
  2. Sodium bicarbonate should be given routinely to correct the acidosis
  3. A fixed-rate IV insulin infusion of 0.1 units/kg/hr is recommended
  4. Venous blood is adequate for assessing pH and bicarbonate
  5. The underlying precipitant (e.g. infection) must be treated
Answer: B. Bicarbonate is NOT indicated — acidosis resolves with fluids and insulin; bicarbonate delays lactate/ketone clearance and may worsen CSF acidosis and hypokalaemia. The others are all correct.

  3. Naloxone

  5. Sodium bicarbonate

Q4. All of the following favor or characterize heat stroke EXCEPT:

  1. Core body temperature above 40 °C
  2. Encephalopathy
  3. Anhydrosis
  4. Profuse sweating with intact thermoregulation
  5. Renal impairment with elevated liver enzymes

Q5. Which of the following statements about drowning is FALSE?
