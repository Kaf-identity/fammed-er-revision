Family Medicine

Chronic disease, common presentations & the principles of general practice.

1. Family Medicine Principles & Consultation Skills

First-contact, continuous, comprehensive, coordinated care — and the evidence base behind it.

Family Medicine (Principles)

Def Specialty of breadth: continuing, comprehensive care across all ages, both sexes, every organ system & disease.
MCQ pearls
  • Core principles = comprehensive, continuous, coordinated (NOT hospital-based / women-children-only / surgical / preventive-only).
  • 5 FP roles = provider, decision maker, communicator, community leader, team leader (surgery is NOT a role).
  • Of all community illness, 3 in 4 self-managed; 1 in 4 reaches PCP; ~1 in 10 of those referred.
  • FP = higher tolerance for uncertainty; country's health quality correlates with primary-care performance.

Evidence-Based Medicine

Def Integration of best research evidence + clinical expertise + patient values (all THREE).
MCQ pearls
  • Strongest → weakest: meta-analysis > RCT > cohort > case-control > cross-sectional > case series > expert opinion (weakest).
  • RCT = gold standard for a treatment; EBM has 3 parts ("research + expertise only" = FALSE).
  • POEM > DOE in reliability (POEM = morbidity/mortality/QoL; DOE = surrogates eg BP, sugar).
  • 5 A's = Ask, Access, Appraise, Apply, Assess; appraise for validity, impact, applicability.
  • Value = quality ÷ cost; useful info = (relevance × validity) ÷ work.

Consultation

Def Central act of medicine = interview (why pt came) + exposition (dx, tx, advice); opening puts pt at ease.
MCQ pearls
  • Patient-centered is preferred (doctor-centered is NOT); explores ideas, concerns, expectations.
  • Management headings = RAPRIOP (Reassurance, Advice, Prescription, Referral, Investigation, Observation, Prevention).
  • Breaking bad news: prepare → what pt knows → warning shot → allow denial → explain → plan → offer availability.

Communication

Def Multi-level info exchange; aims = be heard/accepted, be understood, get action.
MCQ pearls
  • 93% non-verbal (55% body language + 38% tone); only 7% words. When incongruous, non-verbal is more truthful.
  • History gives correct dx in >50% of pts, up to 80% in primary care; video = method of choice to monitor skills.
  • ~90% of doctor complaints relate to dx (mostly history, 82%); doctor interrupts after 18 sec.

Counselling

Def Structured pt education + behaviour-change: introduce → assess knowledge → explain disease → lifestyle then drugs.
MCQ pearls
  • ~50% of medical advice not followed; compliance improves when pt feels in control & advice fits health beliefs.
  • Health Belief Model = motivation, perceived vulnerability + seriousness + cost, triggered by cues to action.
  • Cornerstone of counselling = good communication.

Periodic Health Assessment

Def Systematic prevention via immunizations + screening; good screen = sensitive, specific, safe, acceptable, cost-effective.
ScreenStart ageTool / frequency
Cervical21Cytology q3y; +co-test q5y (30–65)
Breast40 (mammo)Mammography yearly (stop 75)
Colorectal45FOBT yearly / colonoscopy q10y

MCQ pearls
  • Cervical at 21 (regardless of sexual activity); mammography at 40; breast self-exam NOT recommended (level D).
  • USPSTF: A/B = recommend, C = individualize, D = against, I = insufficient. Prevention: 1ry before disease, 2ry early detection, 3ry rehab.

Test Yourself — Family Medicine Principles & Consultation Skills

Q1. A medical student is asked to define the core principles that distinguish family medicine from other specialties. Which statement best captures these principles?

  1. A specialty focused on hospital-based acute care
  2. A specialty providing comprehensive, continuous and coordinated care
  3. A specialty limited to women and children
  4. A specialty providing mainly surgical interventions
  5. A specialty focusing only on preventive medicine
Answer: B. FM is defined by comprehensive, continuous, coordinated (and contextual, accountable) care for all ages and both sexes. The others describe narrow or hospital-bound practice, the opposite of FM's breadth.

Q2. All of the following are TRUE regarding evidence-based medicine EXCEPT:

  1. For patients it represents a more effective use of resources
  2. Patient-oriented evidence (POEM) is more reliable than disease-oriented evidence (DOE)
  3. Meta-analysis provides the strongest evidence available in medicine
  4. EBM improves clinicians' confidence in managing clinical situations
  5. EBM is defined as the integration of only two components: best research evidence and clinical expertise
Answer: E. EBM has THREE components — best research evidence, clinical expertise AND patient values. Dropping patient values makes the statement false. A–D are all true.

Q3. A researcher wants the highest level of evidence for the effectiveness of a new antihypertensive drug. Which single study design provides this?

  1. Case-control study
  2. Case series
  3. Cross-sectional study
  4. Randomized controlled trial
  5. Cohort study
Answer: D. The prospective double-blind RCT is the gold standard for therapeutic decisions. Cohort and case-control are observational (lower); case series and cross-sectional are weaker still. A meta-analysis of RCTs would rank higher, but is not a single study design offered here.

Q4. All of the following are TRUE regarding the consultation and communication EXCEPT:

  1. The opening of the consultation is key to putting the patient at ease
  2. More than 80% of diagnoses can be made by history taking alone in primary care
  3. The doctor-centered style is the preferred consultation style
  4. About 93% of communication is non-verbal
  5. Video recording is the method of choice for monitoring consultation skills
Answer: C. The patient-centered style is preferred, not doctor-centered. A, B, D and E are all true per the sheets (history yields the diagnosis in up to 80% in primary care; 93% of communication is non-verbal; video is the monitoring method of choice).

Q5. A 50-year-old asymptomatic woman with no risk factors asks when she should begin screening. According to the periodic health assessment guidance used here, which statement is correct?

  1. Cervical cytology should have begun at age 21
  2. Cervical screening should only begin now at age 50
  3. Mammography is not indicated at any age
  4. Routine breast self-examination is the recommended screening modality
  5. Colorectal cancer screening is not needed until age 60
Answer: A. Cervical screening starts at 21, so by 50 she is well within the screening window. Mammography starts at 40 (so it IS indicated); breast self-exam is level D (recommend against); colorectal screening starts at 45, not 60.

2. Cardiometabolic & Endocrine

The most heavily tested family-medicine block — HTN, DM and dyslipidemia first, then thyroid and obesity.

Hypertension

Definition Sustained BP elevation; primary/essential (95%) vs secondary (5%, curable).
Stage (AHA/ACC)BP
Normal<120 and <80
Elevated120–129 and <80
Stage 1130–139 or 80–89
Stage 2≥140 or ≥90
MCQ pearls
  • First-line: thiazide, ACEI, ARB, CCB (β-blocker 2nd-line).
  • ACEI → dry cough (switch to ARB) + angioedema, hyperkalemia.
  • CCB/amlodipine → bilateral ankle oedema.
  • ACEI/ARB preferred in diabetics/CKD; pregnancy → methyldopa, nifedipine, labetalol.
  • Emergency = ≥180/≥110 + end-organ damage → IV, drop MAP 25% over 1–2 h.

Diabetes Mellitus

Definition Chronic hyperglycemia from absolute (T1, autoimmune) or relative (T2, resistance + β-cell defect) insulin deficiency.
TestDMPrediabetes
HbA1c≥6.5%5.7–6.4%
FPG≥126100–125
OGTT 2-h≥200140–199
Random≥200 + symptoms—
MCQ pearls
  • Metformin first-line: no hypoglycemia, no weight gain (mild loss); S/E GI, B12 deficiency.
  • Sulfonylureas/meglitinides → hypoglycemia + weight gain; SGLT2/GLP-1 → loss.
  • Glycemic control reduces microvascular; BP control reduces micro- AND macrovascular.
  • IHD = MC cause of death; DM = CHD equivalent (LDL <100, often <70).
  • Fundus screen: T1 at 5 yr, T2 at diagnosis.

Dyslipidemia

Definition ↑ total cholesterol and/or LDL-C (primary target) ± ↑ TG; HDL is protective (>60 ↓ risk).
10-yr riskLDL goalDrug threshold
CHD/equivalent (>20%)<100 (prefer <70)≥130
2+ risk factors (10–20%)<130≥160

MCQ pearls
  • Statins = first-line, most effective for LDL; take at night; S/E myopathy, ↑ LFTs.
  • Stop statin if CPK >10× or LFT >3×; C/I in liver disease.
  • TG ≥500 → acute pancreatitis → very-low-fat diet + fibrate/niacin.
  • CHD equivalents: DM, carotid stenosis, PAD, AAA.
  • Secondary causes: hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, alcohol; thiazide/β-blocker/OCP.

Thyroid Disorders

Definition Hypothyroidism (MC cause Hashimoto) vs hyperthyroidism (MC cause Graves).
FeatureHypoHyper

MCQ pearls
  • Subclinical hypothyroidism = ↑ TSH, normal T4 → treat only if TSH >10, pregnant, symptomatic, or +anti-TPO.
  • Pretibial myxedema specific for Graves; diagnosis is biochemical (no routine US).
  • Hypothyroidism → secondary dyslipidemia (EXCEPT-stem trap).

Obesity

Definition Excess body fat; overweight BMI ≥25, obesity ≥30 (I 30–34.9, II 35–39.9, III ≥40).
MCQ pearls
  • Obesity = BMI ≥30 (≥25 overweight) — common distractor swap.
  • Drug if BMI ≥30, or ≥27 + comorbidity: orlistat, GLP-1, phentermine/topiramate. Metformin NOT an FDA weight drug.
  • Bariatric surgery (BMI ≥40, or ≥35 + comorbidity) = most effective.

Test Yourself — Cardiometabolic & Endocrine

Q1. A 58-year-old man started on an antihypertensive develops a persistent dry cough at night that resolves on stopping the drug. Which class is most likely responsible, and what is the best alternative?

  1. Thiazide → switch to CCB
  2. CCB → switch to ACEI
  3. ACE inhibitor → switch to ARB
  4. β-blocker → switch to thiazide
  5. ARB → switch to ACE inhibitor
Answer: C. ACEIs cause a dry cough (and angioedema); ARBs share the mechanism without cough/angioedema. CCBs cause ankle oedema, not cough.

Q2. All of the following are true regarding diabetes mellitus EXCEPT:

  1. Diagnosis can be made if HbA1c is ≥6.5%
  2. Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia
  3. Metformin commonly causes weight gain
  4. BP control reduces both micro- and macrovascular events
  5. A fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL is diagnostic
Answer: C. Metformin is weight-neutral to weight-reducing, never causing gain; sulfonylureas and thiazolidinediones cause weight gain. The others are all correct diagnostic/management facts.

Q3. A 44-year-old man has triglycerides of 820 mg/dL. Besides cardiovascular risk, which acute complication is he at greatest risk of, and what is the priority management?

  1. Acute pancreatitis → very-low-fat diet plus a fibrate
  2. Acute MI → high-intensity statin alone
  3. Gout flare → allopurinol
  4. Hepatic failure → stop all lipid drugs
  5. Stroke → start aspirin
Answer: A. TG ≥500 mg/dL markedly raises acute pancreatitis risk; management is a very-low-fat diet with fibrate or niacin to lower TG first. Statins are most effective for LDL, not the priority here.

Q4. Which of the following is FALSE regarding hypothyroidism?

  1. The most common cause is Hashimoto thyroiditis
  2. Laboratory findings are ↑ TSH and ↓ free T4
  3. It can cause secondary hyperlipidemia
  4. Subclinical disease shows ↑ TSH with normal T3/T4
  5. Routine thyroid ultrasound is always required to confirm it
Answer: E. Diagnosis is biochemical (↑ TSH, ↓ T4); ultrasound/radionuclide scanning is not routinely needed. The other statements are all true.

Q5. A 60-year-old man with type 2 diabetes and hypertension has an LDL of 162 mg/dL. Which statement about his lipid management is most appropriate?

  1. His LDL goal is <160 mg/dL because he has no symptoms
  2. His LDL goal is <100 mg/dL (or <70) as diabetes is a CHD equivalent, and a statin is first-line
  3. A fibrate is the first-line agent for elevated LDL
  4. Statins should be taken in the morning for best effect
  5. Lifestyle change alone for 3 months is sufficient before any drug
Answer: B. Diabetes is a CHD equivalent, so the LDL goal is <100 (often <70); statins are first-line and most effective for LDL. Fibrates target TG; statins are taken at night.

3. Common Symptom Presentations

High-frequency primary-care complaints — when to reassure, when to investigate.

Dyspepsia

Definition Upper abdominal pain/discomfort (± fullness, nausea, heartburn). 75% functional, 25% organic.
Endoscopy≥60 y<60 y
IndicationAllOnly if ALARM feature
MCQ pearls
  • ALARM: IDA, haematemesis/melena, wt loss >3 kg, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, FHx GI malignancy, mass.
  • Urea breath test = most accurate non-invasive H. pylori test; CLO/rapid urease is invasive (biopsy).
  • <60 + no alarm → empirical PPI + H. pylori test; triple therapy = PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin ×1 wk.
  • Normal endoscopy does NOT exclude GERD. Functional dyspepsia = diagnosis of exclusion (Rome IV).
  • H. pylori = MC cause of PUD.

Upper Respiratory Tract Infections (URTIs)

Definition Mucosal inflammation above carina; 70–80% viral — no antibiotics for common cold.
FeatureViral pharyngitisBacterial (GABHS)
OnsetGradual; cough, rhinorrhea, conjunctivitisAbrupt; fever, exudates, tender anterior nodes, no cough

MCQ pearls
  • EBV mono: posterior nodes, palatal petechiae, hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Sinusitis antibiotics if ≥10 d, severe, or "double-sickening".
  • Croup = parainfluenza, steeple sign; epiglottitis = HiB, drooling/tripod, thumb sign (airway emergency).

Fatigue

Definition Non-specific; only ~15% organic cause; psychological role large.
MCQ pearls
  • Screen: CBC, electrolytes, glucose, BUN/Cr, TSH (PPD not routine).
  • CFS/ME = >6 mo, NOT relieved by rest + post-exertional malaise.
  • Mgmt: aerobic exercise beats rest, CBT; stimulants NOT long-term.

Anemia

  • B12 def = macrocytic + neuro signs; folate = same without neuro. Replace B12 first.
  • Colonoscopy if IDA & >60; iron Rx ×3 mo past Hb correction.

Test Yourself — Common Symptom Presentations

Q1. A 38-year-old man, non-smoker, has a 6-week history of epigastric burning relieved by food. He has no weight loss, no vomiting, no dysphagia, and a normal examination apart from mild epigastric tenderness. Which is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Immediate upper GI endoscopy
  2. Empirical PPI plus testing for H. pylori
  3. Barium swallow
  4. Start a tricyclic antidepressant
  5. Reassure with no investigation or treatment
Answer: B. <60 y with no alarm features → start empirical PPI while testing for H. pylori. Endoscopy (A) is reserved for age ≥60 or alarm features. TCA (D) is for failed eradication / functional dyspepsia.

Q2. All of the following are correct regarding H. pylori and dyspepsia EXCEPT:

  1. The urea breath test is the most accurate non-invasive test
  2. The rapid urease (CLO) test is an invasive test performed on biopsy
  3. First-line eradication is one week of triple therapy (PPI + two antibiotics)
  4. A normal upper endoscopy excludes a diagnosis of GERD
  5. H. pylori is the most common cause of peptic ulcer disease
Answer: D. A normal endoscopy does NOT exclude GERD (non-erosive reflux exists). All other statements are true.

Q3. A 66-year-old man presents with a new headache that is worse at night and associated with early-morning vomiting. Which is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Migraine
  2. Tension headache
  3. Cluster headache
  4. Space-occupying lesion (brain tumour)
  5. Sinusitis
Answer: D. New headache after age 50, worsening, worse at night with morning vomiting = SNNOOP10 red flags suggesting raised ICP / tumour → neuroimaging. Migraine and tension lack these alarm features.

Q4. A 60-year-old woman has recurrent episodes of vertigo each lasting a few hours, accompanied by tinnitus and progressive hearing loss. Which is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo
  2. Vestibular neuritis
  3. Meniere's disease
  4. Acoustic neuroma
  5. Vestibular migraine
Answer: C. The triad of episodic vertigo (1–8 h) + tinnitus + hearing loss is classic Meniere's. BPPV is brief (seconds, positional) and vestibular neuritis spares hearing.

Q5. A 22-year-old woman has fatigue and pallor; her film shows microcytic hypochromic anemia with a Mentzer index of 18, low ferritin and raised TIBC. All of the following are true regarding her condition EXCEPT:

  1. Iron deficiency anemia is the most likely diagnosis
  2. A Mentzer index >13 supports IDA over β-thalassemia trait
  3. The most common underlying cause in a menstruating woman is chronic blood loss
  4. Ferritin is typically elevated in this condition
  5. Iron therapy should continue ~3 months after the Hb normalises
Answer: D. In IDA ferritin is LOW (depleted stores). High/normal ferritin would instead suggest thalassemia or anemia of chronic disease. All other statements are correct.

4. Musculoskeletal & Minor Injuries

Back pain and outpatient wound care — know the red flags, suture table and tetanus rules.

mdash; know the red flags, suture table and tetanus rules.

Back Pain

Definition Pain from spinal muscles, nerves, bones or joints; ~90% benign & self-limiting (resolve <4 wks). Mechanical = worse with activity, better with rest; red-flag = constant/night pain.
Red flags Age <20 or >50; cancer hx; night/rest pain; fever/weight loss; IVDU/immunosuppression; trauma; steroids; pulsatile mass (AAA). Cauda equina = saddle anaesthesia, ↓sphincter tone, bladder/bowel dysfunction — emergency.
MCQ pearls
  • Commonest non-mechanical spinal cause = metastasis; commonest overall = strain/sprain.
  • SLR (same side) sensitive; crossed SLR specific. Disc level: L4 squat, L5 dorsum of foot, S1 plantarflexion.
  • No red flags → don't image; image only if persists >4–6 wks. Tx: NSAIDs + stay active, avoid bed rest.

Minor Injuries

Definition Outpatient lacerations, abrasions, burns & punctures. Key first step = irrigation & cleaning (saline/tap water = antiseptic).
MCQ pearls
  • Suture removal: face 3–5 d (5-0/6-0); scalp/trunk 7 d; over joints 10–14 d; palms/soles 14–21 d (latest).
  • Tetanus toxoid: <3 doses/unknown → give for any wound; ≥3 doses → clean if >10 yrs, dirty if >5 yrs.
  • Tetanus IG: only dirty wound + under-vaccinated (<3 doses/unknown).
  • Sprain = ligament, strain = muscle/tendon → RICE. Severe pain alone is NOT a red flag (bleeding/deformity/loss of function are).

Test Yourself — Musculoskeletal & Minor Injuries

Q1. A <45-year-old man> develops acute low back pain after lifting a heavy box. He has no neurological deficit, no red flags, and is systemically well. Which is the most appropriate initial management?

  1. Strict bed rest for 2 weeks
  2. Urgent lumbar MRI
  3. Scheduled NSAIDs and continue normal activity as tolerated
  4. Lumbar traction
  5. Opioids with full immobilisation
Answer: C. First-line is regular analgesia (NSAIDs) plus staying active; prolonged bed rest is harmful and MRI is not indicated without red flags or persistence >4–6 weeks.

Q2. What is the commonest cause of non-mechanical spinal low back pain?

  1. Disc prolapse
  2. Inflammatory arthropathy
  3. Metastasis
  4. Referred pain
  5. Musculoskeletal strain and sprain
Answer: C. Malignancy/metastasis (~0.7%) is the commonest non-mechanical spinal cause. Strain/sprain is mechanical; referred pain is non-spinal.

Q3. All of the following are red flags in low back pain EXCEPT:

  1. Saddle anaesthesia with urinary incontinence
  2. Pain that improves with rest and worsens with activity
  3. Age over 50 with a history of cancer
  4. Night or rest pain with weight loss
  5. A pulsatile abdominal mass
Answer: B. Pain relieved by rest is the hallmark of benign mechanical back pain. The others (cauda equina, malignancy, AAA) are red flags requiring further evaluation.

Q4. A <24-year-old> healthy man has a superficial laceration from a clean kitchen knife and received a tetanus booster 4 years ago. What is the appropriate tetanus management?

  1. Tetanus toxoid booster only
  2. Tetanus immunoglobulin only
  3. Both toxoid and immunoglobulin
  4. No tetanus prophylaxis needed
  5. Antibiotics only
Answer: D. Clean wound, fully vaccinated (≥3 doses) and last dose <10 years ago — no prophylaxis required. IG is reserved for dirty wounds in under-vaccinated patients.

Q5. Which of the following is FALSE regarding minor wound care and suturing?

  1. Facial wounds use 5-0 or 6-0 sutures removed at 3–5 days
  2. Sutures over palms/soles are removed latest, at 14–21 days
  3. Severe pain on its own is a red flag mandating referral
  4. The most important first step in wound care is irrigation and cleaning
  5. Tap water or sterile saline is as effective as antiseptic for irrigation
Answer: C. Severe pain alone is not a red flag — uncontrolled bleeding, deformity, loss of function and inability to see wound depth are. The other statements match the suture table and irrigation evidence.

Test Yourself — Women's Health & Geriatrics

Q1. According to the Jordanian National Program, routine annual mammography for breast-cancer screening should begin at what age?

  1. 25 years
  2. 30 years
  3. 35 years
  4. 40 years
  5. 50 years
Answer: D. The national program starts annual mammograms at age 40. (50 is the typical USPSTF figure, but the program/sheet uses 40.)

Q2. A 22-year-old sexually active woman asks when she should start cervical cancer (Pap) screening. Per USPSTF, the correct answer is:

  1. At first intercourse
  2. At age 21 years
  3. At age 26 years
  4. At age 30 years
  5. Screening is not recommended at her age
Answer: B. Cervical cytology begins at age 21 regardless of sexual-activity onset. hrHPV/co-testing is added from age 30.

Q3. All of the following are true regarding fibroadenoma EXCEPT:

  1. It is always unilateral
  2. It can calcify
  3. It is benign
  4. It can involute over time
  5. It is typically mobile on examination
Answer: A. Fibroadenomas may be bilateral or multiple — they are NOT always unilateral. All other statements (benign, mobile, can calcify, can involute) are correct.

Q4. Which of the following techniques is NOT used in routine screening for breast cancer?

  1. Mammography
  2. Breast ultrasound
  3. Clinical breast examination
  4. MRI
  5. Breast self-examination
Answer: D. MRI is reserved for high-risk patients, not routine screening. Mammography, ultrasound, CBE and BSE are the routine modalities.

Q5. An 82-year-old hospitalised post-operative man develops acute confusion that worsens at night, with a fluctuating and markedly impaired ability to sustain attention. Which is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Alzheimer's dementia
  2. Delirium
  3. Major depression
  4. Vascular dementia
  5. Lewy body dementia
Answer: B. Acute onset, fluctuating course and markedly impaired attention define delirium (affects ~60% of older surgical inpatients). Dementia is insidious with preserved early attention and a stable daily course.

Test Yourself — Resuscitation & Trauma

Q1. A 30-year-old man is brought in after a road traffic accident with a cervical collar in place and a systolic BP of 80 mmHg. Two attempts at peripheral IV access have failed. Which is the most appropriate next step for vascular access?

  2. Subclavian central venous catheter

Answer: C. After failed peripheral access, intraosseous (anteromedial tibia below the tuberosity) is the rapid second line; central lines (CVCs) are to be avoided in acute trauma resuscitation per the sheet.

Q2. All of the following are components of the circulation assessment in a trauma patient EXCEPT:

  1. Capillary refill time
  2. Blood pressure
  3. Heart rate
  4. Glasgow Coma Scale
  5. Urine output
Answer: D. GCS belongs to Disability, not Circulation. Circulation assessment = peripheral colour, CRT, HR, peripheral temperature, BP, and urine output.

Q3. A 28-year-old man is stabbed in the left chest. He is in severe respiratory distress with hypotension, distended neck veins, a trachea deviated to the right, and absent breath sounds on the left. Which is the most appropriate immediate management?

  2. Needle decompression followed by chest tube
  3. Three-sided occlusive dressing
  4. Emergency thoracotomy in the ED
  5. Intubation and positive-pressure ventilation alone
Answer: B. Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis — decompress immediately (needle) then chest tube; do not delay for CXR (A). A three-sided dressing (C) is for an open/sucking wound.

Q4. Regarding shock in trauma, which of the following is FALSE?

  1. Hypovolaemic shock is the most common type
  2. Narrowed pulse pressure is an early sign
  3. Hot, dry, flushed skin is a typical feature of hypovolaemic shock

Q5. A 60-year-old man collapses; the monitor shows ventricular fibrillation and two DC shocks have failed to achieve ROSC. After the next shock, which medication should be added?

  3. Amiodarone
  4. Magnesium sulphate
  5. Sodium bicarbonate
Answer: C. In shockable rhythms, epinephrine is given after 2 shocks and an antiarrhythmic (amiodarone or lidocaine) after 3 shocks — i.e. after two unsuccessful DC shocks.

10. Metabolic & Endocrine Emergencies

Glucose and potassium crises seen in the ED — diagnose on numbers, treat in the right order.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Definition Insulin deficiency → hyperglycaemia + ketonaemia + metabolic acidosis.
Criteria & key rules
  • Diagnosis (all 3): glucose >11.0 (or known DM); ketones >3.0 (or ketonuria >+2); HCO₃ <15 / pH <7.3.
  • K⁺ rule: if K⁺ <3.5 → replace potassium BEFORE insulin (insulin drives K⁺ in → arrhythmia). Plasma K⁺ normal/high at presentation despite total-body depletion.
  • Order: Fluids → fix K⁺ → insulin 0.1 u/kg/hr (fixed) → treat cause. Venous gas suffices.
FeatureDKAHHS
Glucose>11 (often high)Very high (>30)
Ketones / acidosisPresent; pH <7.3Minimal; pH >7.3
Onset / typeHours; T1DMDays; T2DM, elderly
Flag Bicarbonate NOT indicated (delays ketone clearance, CSF acidosis).

Hypoglycaemia

Definition CBG <4.0 mmol/L in a diabetic needing treatment.
Buzz
  • Whipple's triad: hypoglycaemic symptoms + low measured glucose + relief on correction.
  • Tx: conscious → oral carb/gel; IV access → IV 10% dextrose (50% causes inflammation); no access → IM glucagon. Always follow with complex carb.
  • Glucagon less effective in T2DM and depleted-glycogen states (alcohol, cachexia).

Hyperkalaemia

Definition Serum K⁺ >5.5 mEq/L (severe ≥6.5); arrhythmia/arrest risk.
Buzz
  • ECG sequence: peaked T waves → flat P / wide QRS → sine wave / arrest.
  • Tx order: calcium gluconate (stabilise myocardium, does NOT lower K⁺) → shift (insulin/glucose, neb B-agonist, bicarb) → remove (furosemide, binder, dialysis).
  • Culprit drugs: spironolactone, ACEi/ARB, NSAIDs. Pseudohyperkalaemia = falsely high sample.

Q1. A 19-year-old with type 1 diabetes is unwell with vomiting. Venous gas: glucose 28 mmol/L, pH 7.18, HCO₃ 11 mmol/L, blood ketones 4.2 mmol/L, K⁺ 3.2 mmol/L. Which is the most appropriate first step in management?

  2. Give IV sodium bicarbonate to correct the acidosis

  4. Administer IM glucagon

Q2. All of the following are diagnostic criteria for DKA EXCEPT:

Q3. A 70-year-old on insulin is found drowsy; CBG 2.1 mmol/L. He has IV access and responds poorly. Which is the most appropriate treatment?

  2. IV 50% dextrose by rapid bolus
  3. IV 10% dextrose (~100 ml)
  4. IM glucagon as sole therapy
  5. Withhold treatment and recheck in 1 hour
Answer: C. With IV access and reduced consciousness, IV 10% dextrose is preferred; 50% is hypertonic and causes inflammation. Oral is unsafe if not fully cooperative; glucagon alone is less reliable and risks recurrence without follow-up carbohydrate.

Q4. A 60-year-old with CKD has K⁺ 6.8 mEq/L and peaked T waves on ECG. Which is the most appropriate first intervention?

  2. IV insulin with glucose
  3. IV calcium gluconate
  4. Oral potassium binder (Kayexalate)
  5. Furosemide
Answer: C. Calcium gluconate stabilises the myocardium first. Shifting agents (insulin/glucose, B-agonist, bicarbonate) come next, then removal (diuretic, binder, dialysis). Calcium itself does not lower the serum K⁺.

Q5. Regarding the management of DKA, which statement is FALSE?

  1. Fluid replacement is the most important initial intervention
  2. Sodium bicarbonate should be given routinely to correct the acidosis
  3. A fixed-rate IV insulin infusion of 0.1 units/kg/hr is recommended
  4. Venous blood is adequate for assessing pH and bicarbonate
  5. The underlying precipitant (e.g. infection) must be treated
Answer: B. Bicarbonate is NOT indicated — acidosis resolves with fluids and insulin; bicarbonate delays lactate/ketone clearance and may worsen CSF acidosis and hypokalaemia. The others are all correct.

Q3. A farm worker presents with miosis, salivation, lacrimation, bradycardia and muscle fasciculations after spraying pesticide. Which is the most appropriate antidote combination?

Q4. All of the following favor or characterize heat stroke EXCEPT:

Answer: D. Heat stroke is complete thermoregulatory failure (triad: core >40 °C, encephalopathy, anhydrosis) with multi-organ involvement. Preserved sweating with core <40 °C describes heat exhaustion, not heat stroke.

  3. The volume aspirated is usually large, so draining water from the lungs is essential
  4. Patients should be extracted from the water in a horizontal position
  5. Asymptomatic patients with normal CXR and ABG may be discharged after 6 hours
Answer: C. The aspirated volume is small (<4 mL/kg), so attempting to drain water from the lungs is ineffective. The other statements are all correct per the drowning learning points.

12. GI & Abdominal Emergencies

Resuscitate first, find the source second — and never miss a variceal bleed or a vascular catastrophe.

Test Yourself — GI & Abdominal Emergencies

Q1. A <56-year-old man> presents with four large fresh-red vomits and black tarry stools for 12 hours. He is clammy with cool peripheries, pulse 130 bpm and BP 70/40 mmHg, and has known ischaemic heart disease. Which is the most appropriate initial priority?

  1. Urgent upper GI endoscopy
  2. High-flow oxygen with two large-bore IV cannulae and fluid resuscitation
  3. IV omeprazole 40 mg
  4. IV terlipressin 2 mg bolus
  5. Insert a Minnesota tube
Answer: B. Resuscitate first — O2, ≥16G access, bloods/cross-match, crystalloid while awaiting blood. Endoscopy (A) comes only after resuscitation; PPI (C) is not given pre-endoscopy; terlipressin (D) is for suspected variceal bleeds; the tube (E) is a later rescue.

Q2. A <45-year-old man with alcoholic liver disease> arrives after several large haematemeses, clammy with pulse 120 and BP 80/40 mmHg, jaundiced with ascites. After resuscitation, which drug combination should be given in the ED?

  1. IV omeprazole + tranexamic acid
  2. IV vitamin K + fresh frozen plasma
  3. Terlipressin + ceftriaxone
  4. Octreotide + IV PPI
  5. Tranexamic acid alone
Answer: C. Suspected variceal bleed → terlipressin (34% mortality RRR) plus prophylactic antibiotics in the ED. PPI (A/D) is post-endoscopy only; vitamin K and tranexamic acid (A/B/E) have no evidence here.

Q3. All of the following regarding upper GI bleeding are correct EXCEPT:

  1. Peptic ulcer disease is the most common cause
  2. A Glasgow-Blatchford score of 0 allows consideration of early discharge with outpatient endoscopy
  3. Red cells should be transfused once haemoglobin falls below 7 g/dL (below 9 g/dL in unstable coronary disease)
  4. IV proton pump inhibitor should be started in the ED before endoscopy
  5. Endoscopy is the gold standard for diagnosis and treatment once the patient is resuscitated
Answer: D. NICE advises PPI should NOT be commenced pre-endoscopy (no mortality/re-bleed benefit, masks the source) — it is a post-endoscopy drug. All others are true.

Q4. A <60-year-old man> has 12 hours of severe left lower abdominal pain with nausea and two episodes of vomiting that briefly relieved the pain, plus new constipation over recent months. Examination shows diffuse abdominal rigidity and distension. Which is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Acute appendicitis
  2. Renal colic
  3. Intestinal obstruction
  4. Acute pancreatitis
  5. Pelvic inflammatory disease
Answer: C. Colicky pain, vomiting relieving the pain, distension and a change in bowel habit point to bowel obstruction. Appendicitis (A) localises to the RLQ; renal colic (B) radiates loin-to-groin; pancreatitis (D) is epigastric.

Q5. Regarding the assessment of acute abdominal pain, which statement is FALSE?

  1. "Pain out of proportion to examination findings" should raise suspicion of mesenteric ischaemia
  2. Myocardial infarction can present as acute epigastric pain
  3. Systolic blood pressure does not fall until blood loss reaches 30–40% of blood volume
  4. The presence or absence of fever reliably distinguishes surgical from medical disease
  5. In the elderly, serious abdominal disease may be present despite normal vital signs and a normal white cell count
Answer: D. Temperature is neither sensitive nor specific and cannot distinguish surgical from medical disease — especially in the elderly, who may be hypothermic with serious infection. The rest are true.
